INTRAMUROS

Nestled in the heart of Manila, the walled city of Intramuros once served as the powerful political hub of the Philippines during its Spanish colonial era. Encircled by imposing bastions, moats, and cannons, the 64-hectare enclave was a fortified stronghold that sheltered the grand homes of the Spanish elite, majestic churches, peaceful monasteries, bustling plazas, and prestigious institutions like the original University of Santo Tomas.

INTRAMUROS

‹ ›

It contained Fort Santiago, a citadel strategically placed near Manila Bay. Originally a wooden fort built by Rajah Sulayman, the sultan of Manila, it later became a key outpost in the Spanish Galleon trade, connecting Manila to Acapulco for more than two centuries. However, the fort's most somber claim to fame is its role as the final prison of the Philippine national hero Jose Rizal, who was held there before his execution in 1896.

Over the centuries, Intramuros stood resilient against waves of attackers—Chinese pirates, Dutch invaders, and British, American, and Japanese forces. Yet, it was only in the dying days of World War II that Intramuros finally fell, after the Japanese Imperial Army made their last stand during the fierce Battle of Manila. Nearly all of the city’s buildings were reduced to rubble, leaving only the historic San Agustin Church standing as a testament to its enduring legacy.

Banaue Rice Terraces

‹ ›

The Banaue Rice Terraces are a National Cultural Treasure of the Philippines and are considered to be the 8th Natural Wonder of the World by many Filipinos, and are located on the mountains of Ifugao and were carved there about 2,000 years ago by the ancestors of the present day Philippines.

The terraces are irrigated by water from catchment areas in the rainforests above it, and is one of the most spectacular engineering feats to have been accomplished in the ancient world. The geographical structure of the Banaue Rice Terraces of Ifugao led to UNESCO’s declaration in 1995 which recognized the place as a World Heritage Site.

Biak na Bato

‹ ›

Biak-na-Bato is a historical site which is located in the municipality of San Miguel, in the province of Bulacan, Philippines. Specifically, it is situated in the Biak-na-Bato National Park, which was declared by Manuel Quezon in 1937 to be a national park in virtue of its association with the history and site of the Biak-na-Bato Republic and is a protected area in the mountainous region of Bulacan.

The Republic of Biak-na-Bato, officially referred to in its constitution as the Republic of the Philippines, was the first republic ever declared in the Philippines by revolutionary leader Emilio Aguinaldo and his fellow revolutionaries. Despite its successes, including the establishment of the Philippines’ first ever constitution, the republic lasted just over a month. It was disestablished by a peace treaty signed by Aguinaldo and the Spanish Governor-General, Fernando Primo de Rivera which included provision for exile of Aguinaldo and key associates to Hong Kong.

Fort Santiago

‹ ›

Fort Santiago was named after Saint James, Slayer of the Moors (Santiago Matamoros), who stands as a historical icon in Manila. It was built in 1571 by Spanish colonizers on a strategic site previously fortified by Rajah Soliman, the fort underwent transformations after damage in 1574 and was rebuilt with stone between 1589 and 1592. Over the centuries, Fort Santiago served as a military headquarters for Spanish, British (1762–1764), American (1898–1946), and Japanese (1942–1945) forces.

During World War II, it became a grim site where the Japanese secret police, Kempeitai, imprisoned and tortured Filipinos. The fort was heavily damaged by American forces in 1945, leaving only remnants of its original structure. After the war, it was restored as a public park, and in 1951, the Philippines declared it a National Monument and Shrine of Freedom in honor of Dr. José Rizal and others who were imprisoned or martyred there.

Fort Santiago was recognized as a National Cultural Treasure by the National Museum of the Philippines in 2014, it now stands as a memorial to Filipino resilience and sacrifice.
